In this section, we will cover every part of creating a successful trading plan for trading Biotech Catalysts such as FDA Approvals, Phase Data Releases, and Presentations.

## Step 1: Find & Evaluate Biotech Plays

In order to trade Biotech plays, you are first going to want to find the dates at which catalysts will be happening.

Resources: https://www.biopharmcatalyst.com, https://www.fdatracker.com/fda-calendar/, https://www.rttnews.com/corpinfo/fdacalendar.aspx

These websites may or may not offer premium services, but the free services will get you everything you need. We do not endorse any premium biotech catalyst services and recommend you stay away from spending money to find public dates.

Once you have found Biotech dates, it is time to filter through them and find ones that are relevant. You will find several main categories of FDA dates.

### A. PDUFA (Prescription Drug User Fee Act)

This is by far the most impactful FDA date for most biotech plays. This is when a company is waiting on the FDA to give them a decision on an approval for a drug to be sold. A PDUFA designation means that the company has gone through enough phase trials already that they are essentially at the final stage of entering the market. (Note: There is about an 80% rate of approval once a drug reaches PDUFA).

### B. Phase Data 1-3 Data Releases

These are the different "phase trials" all drugs must go through before getting to the PDUFA stage.

- **Phase 1:** Consists of testing the drug product in a small number of humans, normally healthy volunteers, to determine preliminary safety and tolerable dose range.
- **Phase 2:** They conduct studies in a limited patient population to evaluate the effectiveness of the drug
- **Phase 3:** Additional Testing For Clinical Safety

### C. Phase Presentations

This is when companies present their findings from their Phase Trials. It gives companies an opportunity to bolster their share price, draw in investors, and at times create media pumps. This is often not a huge catalyst, however, it serves to bring in investor interest and thus volume.

## Step 2: Write Down The Dates

Make a calendar of relevant Biotech Plays for the upcoming month. This will allow you to keep a close eye on them in the week before and during the catalyst. Since a lot of Biotech opportunities start drawing investor interest as far as 2-3 weeks before a catalyst, this is an important thing to keep track of.

## Step 3: Research Company & Look For Previous Patterns

Once you have found some tickers, do some due diligence and look into its past.

Focus On:

**A. Recent Insider Activity** - You should focus on finding what and when the insiders are moving shares

**B. Previous Reactions To Similar News Events** - If you are looking to trade an FDA approval (for example), look to see how your ticker has reacted to previous FDA approvals

**C. Who Are The Institutional Holders?** - Look to spot big name institutional holders. Certain institutional holders such as Blackrock have a high success rate.

**D. How Relevant Is The Announcement?** - How important is this announcement for their company? A lot of these small biotech plays are completely built around the success of one drug. This means that any news regarding that one drug is going to have a much larger impact on the share price.

**E. Analyst Price Targets** - Where are the analyst price targets? Are they above or below the current trading price?

## Step 4: Form A Hypothesis

Based on your research in Step 3, go forward and form a hypothesis on whether or not you think the drug is going to be passed. A hypothesis is essentially an educated guess as to whether or not it will be approved. An inaccurate hypothesis doesn't hurt you but an accurate hypothesis helps you. In the beginning, most of your hypotheses will be inaccurate but they will aide you in trading these plays and understanding what is going on.

(Inaccurate hypotheses don't hurt you because you only act on a hypothesis if its CONFIRMED as in Step 6)

## Step 5: Identify Early Warning Signs

**A. Pre-Announcement Run-up** - This can be a sign that insiders or institutional investors are buying in before the announcement.

**B. Pre-Announcement Breakdown** - This can be a sign that insiders or institutional investors are selling before the announcement.

**C. No Reaction** - Alternatively, you may find that there is no reaction whatsoever. If the stock price is flat running up to an announcement, that might mean that the announcement is either irrelevant to most investors, or that the stock isn't getting much media attention. Both of these things are bad because if they can't get media attention, you won't be able to trade off their price action. So make sure that you are seeing some signs of at least increased volume running up to an announcement. If not, it is likely because you chose a catalyst that is irrelevant to the company and thus need to return to step 3.

## Step 6: Enter When It Makes Sense

A.) Confirms Your Hypothesis or B.) Makes Sense Based on Price Action Elevating Factors

- A. Getting In At A PRICE STRENGTH confirmation for pre-anticipation runup/breakdown
- B. Waiting for Price Strength AFTER approval for post-announcement runup/breakdown

## Step 7: Do NOT hold and Hope.

Anyone can enter a stock at confirmation, but you also need to understand where to exit.

Do not be this sappy sop. Holding and Hoping will make you a MISERABLE trader and will destroy your account.

Exit at the moment your hypothesis is no longer correct (price strength reverses, elevating factors turn into deprecating ones)
