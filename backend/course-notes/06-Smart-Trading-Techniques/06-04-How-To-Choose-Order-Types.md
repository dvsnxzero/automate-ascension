# How To Choose Order Types

**Module:** Smart Trading Techniques  
**Lesson:** 4 of 5

---

When you make a trade, you need to choose an order type.

Here are the options:

- **Market Order** -> Buy Or Sell At CURRENT Price
- **Limit Order** -> Buy Or Sell At SPECIFIC Price
- **Stop Order** -> Buy Or Sell At CURRENT Price After A Specific Period Is Reached

(Note: "Stop" Order is often referred to as a "stop loss" order in popular culture)

---

## Market Order Examples

**Scenario 1:** If you choose a market order to sell your shares, it will sell at the next available market price. That means that if the price is moving down rapidly, you might get executed out a lot lower than you wanted (or higher, if it's going up).

**Scenario 2:** If you choose a market order to buy your shares, it will buy at the next available market price. That means that if the price is moving up rapidly, you might get executed in a lot higher than you wanted (or lower, if it's going down).

**Pro of Market Orders:**
- Highest chance of being executed (and fastest). For example, if you want to sell your shares at a market price of $50, but the stock is decreasing quickly, you may get sold out at $35. The reverse is also true.

---

## Limit Order Examples

**Scenario 1:** If you choose a limit order to sell your shares at $50, that means that it will only sell your shares if it is able to do so at $50 (or higher).

**Scenario 2:** If you choose a limit order to buy shares at $50, that means that it will only buy shares at $50 or lower.

**Pros of Limit Orders:**
- You get what you want. Your execution point will not be a surprise, it will only execute if it fits the requirement of your order.

**Cons of Limit Orders:**
- Since you have set specific criteria, if shares aren't available at your price point then you may never get executed. This can be a disaster if you are trying to cut losses quickly in a stock that is moving down rapidly. It can also mean missing out on getting executed into good positions.

---

## Stop Order Examples

**Scenario 1:** If you choose a stop order to sell your shares only if it breaks below $50, once it breaks below that level it'll automatically place a market order to sell shares.

**Scenario 2:** If you choose a stop order to buy shares only if it breaks above $50, once it breaks above that level it'll automatically place a market order to buy shares.

**Pros of Stop Order:**
- You are much more likely to get executed as compared to a limit order. This is great, because it means you are much less likely to be stuck holding a position if something drops rapidly.
- This is a great way to help "protect" your capital & catch breakouts by only executing if it crosses below or above a certain point

**Cons of Stop Order:**
- Since a Stop Order actually creates a market order once activated, that means that you can end up being executed at a different price point than you wanted. The idea that a "stop" order guarantees protection of your downside is untrue - it simply means that it'll execute you as a market order at that specified level.
