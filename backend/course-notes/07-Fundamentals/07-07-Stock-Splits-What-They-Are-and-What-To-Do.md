# Stock Splits: What They Are & What To Do

**Module:** Fundamentals: What They Tell Us & How To USE them  
**Lesson:** 7 of 12

---

A company may end up splitting their share price for various reasons. This means they are essentially increasing the number of shares that each shareholder has.

## 2 for 1 Split

In a 2 for 1 split, each share you have will be split into 2 shares.

Example: If you have 1 share of ZipPharma worth $100, you now have 2 shares worth $50 each.

## 4 for 1 Split

In a 4 for 1 split, each share you have will be split into 4 shares.

Example: If you have 1 share of ZipPharma worth $100, you now have 4 shares worth $25 each.

This is pretty intuitive stuff, and the value of the grand total of your stocks will stay the same after the split. The splits aren't limited to just "2-1 and 4-1," they can also be 10-1, 50-1, and so on and so forth. However, investors may exit or enter causing fluctuation afterwards. Generally speaking - splits of this nature are often seen as a positive for holders because they now have more shares and thus a decrease in individual share price, which allows investors to take positions more easily.

---

## Reverse Stock Splits

On the flip side, a company can also reverse stock split. A reverse stock split is when a company chooses to decrease the number of shares publicly traded by combining them. This is almost always a bad sign because it means that companies want to get their individual share prices up (at least numerically).

### 1 for 10 Split

In a 1 for 10 split, your 10 shares will turn into one share.

Example: If you have 10 shares of ZipPharma worth total $100, you now have 1 share worth $100.

### 1 for 100 Split

In a 1 for 100 split, your 100 shares will turn into one share.

Example: If you have 100 shares of ZipPharma worth total $100, you now have 1 share worth $100.

**Why would a company want to do this?** Well, because many exchanges (NASDAQ, NYSE, Etc) have minimum per-share requirements to be listed. So a company that is rapidly dropping, is likely going to explore splitting their share price to prevent delisting.

**What usually happens?** Most of the time the reverse stock split delays the inevitable. The fact is that a rapidly decreasing company is still losing value overall - artificially changing the cost per share only creates a surface level change to price. So when you see a reverse stock split, understand that the majority of the time it is going to continue running down overall after the split. A few times, however, the reverse stock split will buy the company enough time to turn things around. However, we don't recommend holding to find out.

**Know this:** Most brokers will charge you a fee for holding through any type of stock split. Make sure to check with your broker to see how much your fee will be if you decide to hold.
