# SEC Halts: What To Do

**Module:** Fundamentals: What They Tell Us & How To USE them  
**Lesson:** 8 of 12

---

One of the most frustrating things that we deal with as traders are SEC Halts (AKA Trading Halts).

This is when the SEC essentially pauses trading for a given security during a certain time period. These can happen any time of the day, and for whatever reason. This means that if you are already in a position, there is absolutely no way to execute out of it until it starts trading again.

## Why Does This Happen?

The SEC will halt a stock before any piece of significant news is released. This could be an FDA Approval, Earnings Release, CEO resigning, Lawsuit etc. This way, the stock price doesn't get affected until after the news comes out.

## How Can You Tell What Stocks Are Being Halted?

You can google "SEC halts" or use the NASDAQ website to scan for trading halts.

On this chart it'll tell you whether or not your stock has been halted. It'll also show you reasons for a halt, under "reason codes."

### Here are the main ones to know:

- **T1 Halt:** Trading is halted pending release of material news. This is the most common type of halt and usually lasts about 10 minutes.
- **T12 Halt:** Trading is halted pending receipt of additional information requested by exchange.

## Examples Of SEC Halts

In 2016, DRYS was halted after a 1,500% increase in share value. As a result, the NASDAQ decided to halt it and requested news to backup the increase. They do this to protect shareholders and interested parties.

In 2019, NBRV was paused right before a positive FDA announcement. Right after it opened, it got beat down massively but then ran up after hours.

## How Long Do They Last?

SEC Halts based on News are supposed to last 10 minutes (5 minutes prior to announcement, 5 minutes after) but in reality SEC Halts can last a lot longer. An SEC Halt where "additional information is requested" can stay paused for hours. Generally, the longer the SEC takes to unpause - the more attention a stock is going to get when it opens. This means that there will likely be more volatility upon open.

## How Do We Trade Them?

Since SEC Halts come in all different forms, the key is to understand the risk beforehand. This means asking yourself whether or not you are willing to be stuck in a position if it is halted. This is risky because if the position gets halted and a piece of news comes out that is damaging, you are going to have a hard time executing out once the SEC reopens the stock. So ask yourself this question ahead of time.

When it comes to "pending on additional information," being stuck in a position can be disastrous. The longer it takes, the more likely the price action is going to be negatively impacted upon open. The best way to play this is by playing off overreaction lows after the announcement.
